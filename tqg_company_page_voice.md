# The Quran Group — Company Page Voice Guide

## What this page is

The Quran Group (TQG) is a UK Community Interest Company (CIC 17039732) providing free online Islamic education globally. 500+ students across 6 continents. 6 volunteer huffaz teachers. 2 scholars. 5-7 free live classes daily (Mon-Sat). Everything is free, always.

The company page is the credibility anchor. Isa's personal account is the growth engine. They serve different purposes and should never sound the same.

---

## Voice

The TQG page speaks as an organisation, not as Isa. It has authority without arrogance. It teaches without preaching. It tells stories about Islamic history, the Quran, and the Sahabah in a way that makes a professional on LinkedIn stop and think.

The tone is warmer than a corporate page but more authoritative than Isa's personal account. Think: a knowledgeable friend who studied this deeply and is sharing what they found.

---

## Content types

### Companion stories (primary content)

Historical posts about Islamic figures: Sahabah, Prophets, scholars. Each post tells one story from one angle with one point. Not a biography. A single moment or decision from that person's life.

The formula: surprising hook about the figure, the story behind it, a line or two connecting it to a broader principle. No explicit "lesson" or "takeaway" paragraph.

Working series: Umar RA (Jerusalem), Khalid RA (battlefield conversion), Uthman RA (Quran compilation), Abu Kadama RA (the boy soldier). Each figure gets multiple posts from different angles over time.

### Quranic content

Ayah-based posts with context. Not just "beautiful verse" posts with stock calligraphy. The ayah should connect to something specific: a historical moment, a principle, a decision someone made.

### Operational updates (rare)

Only when there's something real to announce: new course launch, teacher addition, milestone. Never "we're excited to announce" or "big news" framing. Just state what's happening.

---

## Hooks

The TQG page uses provocative or curiosity hooks. These are the highest performing pattern.

Examples that worked:
- "Dhikr didn't conquer Jerusalem. A man who did dhikr did." (2,754 impressions, 6.46% engagement)
- "Every Quran on earth goes back to one man's decision." (3,188 impressions, 5.77% engagement)
- "Khalid ibn al-Walid RA fought against the Muslims." (542 impressions, 4.98% engagement)
- "A boy showed up to the battlefield and Abu Kadama رضي الله عنه told him to go home." (477 impressions, 4.61% engagement)

Pattern: state something that sounds wrong, contradictory, or incomplete. The reader clicks to find out the rest.

Hooks that underperform: descriptive hooks that explain what the post is about before the reader has a reason to care. "Here's the story of how Umar RA..." doesn't stop scrolling.

---

## Structure

Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Some single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.

No bullet points. No headers. No numbered lists. These are narrative posts.

Arabic text included naturally when quoting Quran or hadith. Always with English translation. The Arabic adds authenticity for Muslim readers. The English makes it accessible to everyone.

Use the Prophet's ﷺ actual words when quoting hadith. Never paraphrase. Never generate reference numbers from memory. If unsure of the exact wording, describe the content without pretending to quote.

---

## Endings

The TQG page can end with a clear statement or principle. Unlike Isa's personal posts which fade out, the company page can land on a line that people screenshot.

Examples:
- "Dhikr without action is comfort. Action without dhikr is arrogance."
- "Your past doesn't disqualify you. Sitting still does."

But never more than one concluding line. Never a summary paragraph. Never "in conclusion." When the point is made, stop.

---

## What never appears on TQG page

- First person singular ("I"). Use "we" for organisational voice or third person for stories.
- Isa's personal stories (those go on his personal account)
- Fundraising asks (unless a specific campaign is live)
- Reposts from Isa's personal account (these get zero algorithmic distribution, confirmed by data)
- Generic ayah graphics with no original writing (these are the lowest effort Islamic content on LinkedIn and only work at scale with large groups)
- Political content (Palestine, geopolitics, government criticism)
- Promotional language ("we're thrilled," "exciting news," "big announcement")

---

## Banned words and phrases

Same banned list as the personal account anti-slop rules. Additionally:

- "We're excited to..." / "We're proud to..." / "We're thrilled to..."
- "Our mission is to..." (show it, don't state it)
- "We believe that..." (demonstrate beliefs through stories, don't declare them)
- "Join us on this journey" / "Come along for the ride"
- "Community" used generically (name the specific group: students, teachers, huffaz)
- "Impact" as a noun ("making an impact")
- "Passionate about"

---

## Islamic content rules

Never preach. The reader should finish a TQG post feeling like they learned something, not like they were lectured.

Historical stories work better than prescriptive content. "Umar RA did this" is more engaging than "You should do this."

Balance: never 3 consecutive posts about the same type of figure (e.g. 3 Sahabah posts). Mix Prophets, scholars, and Sahabah. Use figure gap alerts from the content calendar.

Hadith: always verify against sunnah.com before posting. Include the collection name. Never fabricate or approximate reference numbers.

Quran: use Uthmani script for Arabic text. Sahih International for English translation unless another translation better captures the meaning for a specific post.

---

## Engagement rules

TQG page comments on Isa's personal posts when relevant (builds cross-traffic). Keep these short and warm, not corporate.

Good: "We're glad itekaaf brought you and Isa together. The best ideas start in the most unexpected places. Welcome Sunnah 🐝"

Bad: "Thank you for sharing this inspiring story! We at The Quran Group are committed to..."

Reply to comments on TQG posts briefly and warmly. Never use corporate reply templates.

---

## Images

For companion stories: relevant historical images or landscapes. Masjid Al-Aqsa for Umar RA. Battlefield landscapes for Khalid RA. Ancient manuscript images for Uthman RA.

Never AI-generated images. Never generic Islamic stock photos (crescent moons, prayer beads, generic mosque silhouettes).

For Quranic content: nature scenes that match the ayah theme. Water scenes for ayahs about provision. Desert/mountain for ayahs about patience. Real photography only.

---

## Posting frequency

3 posts per week on the TQG page. All original content.

Never repost from Isa's personal account to TQG page (confirmed zero distribution from analytics).

Space posts across the week. Don't cluster 3 posts in 2 days then go silent for 5.

## The underlying message

Every TQG post carries the same implicit message: Islamic knowledge is for everyone, not the privileged few. The Sahabah were ordinary people who did extraordinary things through commitment and faith. You can do the same. We exist to make that possible.

This is never stated directly. It comes through the stories, the accessibility of the language, and the fact that the organisation itself is free.

## Tone comparison: Personal vs TQG

| Element | Isa's Personal | TQG Page |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Perspective | First person, "I" | Third person or "we" |
| Topics | Personal stories, career, academic | Islamic history, Quran, announcements |
| Emotional range | Vulnerability, frustration, humour | Authority, warmth, wonder |
| Hook style | Scene or moment from his life | Provocative or contradictory historical fact |
| Endings | Fade out, no wrap-up | Can land on a quotable line |
| Islamic content | Natural, woven into personal stories | Central, the whole point |
| Humour | Occasional, self-deprecating | Rare, only when it serves the story |
| CTA | Almost never | Only for sign-ups or course launches |
| Emoji | Very rare (😅 😂) | Almost never |

## The test

Read the draft out loud. Does it sound like:
- A corporate social media team? Rewrite.
- A generic Islamic content page? Rewrite.
- A knowledgeable teacher who happens to be young, sharp, and uninterested in formality? Post it.
